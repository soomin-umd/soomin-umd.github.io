## 💡 Relevance to My Research

- **Intergenerational educational mobility**: Incarcerated populations are disproportionately drawn from low-income, low-education families. Correctional education credentials may represent a critical—if understudied—mechanism for disrupting intergenerational cycles of poverty and limited educational attainment. If credential completion in prison improves economic outcomes, it may also improve the educational environment for the next generation (children of formerly incarcerated parents).
- **First-generation college students (FGCS)**: Many incarcerated individuals are themselves first-generation in any form of postsecondary education. Understanding which credentials "pay off" for this population informs broader questions about whether short-term, non-traditional credentials serve as viable on-ramps for populations historically excluded from higher education—a dynamic shared with FGCS.
- **State financial aid policy**: Correctional education is funded largely through state appropriations and, increasingly, reinstated Pell Grant eligibility (via the FAFSA Simplification Act/Second Chance Pell). This study's findings directly inform whether state investments in prison-based education yield measurable economic returns, which is relevant to policy debates about how states allocate financial aid and workforce development dollars across populations, including traditionally underserved groups.
