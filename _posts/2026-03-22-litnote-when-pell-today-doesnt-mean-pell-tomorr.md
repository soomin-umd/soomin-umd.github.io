## 📌 Core Research Question

How does the **minimum Pell Grant** affect students' academic outcomes, and what methodological challenges arise when evaluating financial aid programs whose **eligibility is dynamic** — meaning students who qualify in one year may not qualify in subsequent years (and vice versa)? The paper interrogates why the Pell Grant has a "mixed evaluation record" despite the broader consensus that need-based aid improves outcomes, pointing to the instability of eligibility status over time as a key analytical complication.

## 🔬 Methodology

While the full text is not available (ahead of print), the title and abstract strongly suggest the authors employ a **regression discontinuity (RD) design** around the **minimum Pell Grant threshold** — a common approach in Pell Grant evaluations that exploits the sharp cutoff in the Expected Family Contribution (EFC) that determines eligibility. The paper's central methodological contribution appears to be highlighting and addressing the problem of **dynamic eligibility**: students near the Pell eligibility margin may cross in and out of eligibility across years due to changes in family income, household size, enrollment intensity, or FAFSA reporting. This creates challenges for:

- **Intent-to-treat (ITT) estimation**, since initial assignment to "treatment" (Pell receipt) does not guarantee sustained treatment in future periods.
- **Interpreting treatment effects at the margin**, since the complier population is inherently unstable.
- Potential **attenuation bias** if researchers assume static eligibility when eligibility is in fact fluid.

The authors likely model or simulate how this dynamic eligibility affects causal estimates and may propose corrections or reframings for RD-based Pell evaluations.
