- **Intergenerational educational mobility**: Racially disparate K-12 funding is a key upstream mechanism shaping who is prepared for and gains access to higher education. If SFRs only slowly reduce racial funding gaps, the intergenerational transmission of educational disadvantage — particularly for Black, Hispanic, and Indigenous families — persists through underresourced schools, affecting college readiness and ultimately mobility.
- **First-generation college students (FGCS)**: FGCS disproportionately come from racially minoritized and low-income communities. The finding that economic progressivity does not automatically translate into racial equity means that even in states with reformed funding formulas, the K-12 schools serving future FGCS of color may remain underfunded, compounding the disadvantages these students carry into postsecondary education.
- **State financial aid policy**: This paper highlights the limits of race-neutral, income-targeted state policy design — a lesson directly transferable to state financial aid. Need-based aid formulas that are economically progressive but race-blind may similarly fail to close racial gaps in college affordability and completion, paralleling the K-12 dynamic documented here. It provides a useful analogy and empirical grounding for arguing that state higher education aid policies should be evaluated for racial equity outcomes, not just income-based progressivity.
